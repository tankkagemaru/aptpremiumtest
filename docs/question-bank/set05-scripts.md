### listening/set05-l3-q15.mp3
- **Voices:** one male and one female, 30s–40s, a friendly but genuine disagreement; the woman sceptical of homework, the man defending it in a reasonable, unheated way. About 90 seconds in total.
- **Pace:** B2, ~160 wpm, natural contractions and quick turn-taking.
- **Script:**
  - Man: Did you read that some schools are planning to scrap homework completely?
  - Woman: I did, and honestly I think they have a point. Most of what my nephew brings home is copying, not thinking. He's still at the kitchen table at nine o'clock, exhausted.
  - Man: I agree that a great deal of it is pointless — copying out definitions teaches nobody anything. Where I'd disagree is getting rid of it altogether. Some subjects simply need practice, and you can't fit all of that into a forty-minute lesson.
  - Woman: Practice, perhaps. But there's a fairness problem as well. Some children have a quiet desk and a parent who can help; others are sharing a room with three brothers and sisters. Homework quietly rewards the lucky ones.
  - Man: I'd say that's an argument for better support, not for abolishing the work itself. A homework club after school would deal with most of it.
  - Woman: And who exactly is going to run this club? Teachers are already working every evening as it is.
  - Man: Volunteers. Older students, retired teachers, parents. It isn't impossible.
  - Woman: Hmm. What I'd really like to see is schools setting far less, and making what's left genuinely worth doing.
  - Man: On that we agree entirely. Quality rather than quantity — that's the answer, whatever else changes.

### listening/set05-l4a-q16.mp3
- **Voices:** one female, 40s–50s, university lecturer, dry and lightly ironic, warming to her argument. About 110 seconds.
- **Pace:** C1, natural pace 160+ wpm, varied intonation; a short pause before "What interests me".
- **Script:** Ask a group of students how they revise, and most will describe the same routine: they read their notes, and then they read them again, perhaps with a highlighter in hand. It feels productive. The page becomes familiar, the sentences begin to flow, and that fluency is quietly mistaken for knowledge. Yet study after study points in an uncomfortable direction. When learners are tested some weeks later, those who simply re-read remember strikingly little, while those who shut the book and tried to recall the material — often getting it wrong at first — remember a great deal more. The act of retrieving information, it seems, is what fixes it in place. Recognising it does almost nothing. What interests me is not the finding itself, which is now thoroughly established, but our stubborn resistance to it. Retrieval practice feels difficult and unrewarding. You sit staring at a blank page, painfully aware of everything you cannot remember, and you conclude that the method is not working. Re-reading, by contrast, feels smooth and reassuring. We are, in short, poor judges of our own learning, and we consistently choose the strategy that feels effective over the one that is. Until teachers make that gap between feeling and fact explicit, students will go on highlighting their notes into the small hours, and wondering why so little of it survives the examination.

### listening/set05-l4b-q17.mp3
- **Voices:** one male, 40s–50s, measured commentator on working life, persuasive and precise. About 110 seconds.
- **Pace:** C1, natural pace 160+ wpm; mark the three reasons clearly with slight pauses.
- **Script:** For thirty years, the advice offered to school leavers was blunt: get a degree, or get left behind. Universities expanded, graduate numbers soared, and practical training was quietly treated as the option for everybody else. Now, in a number of industries, that hierarchy is being reconsidered. Engineering firms, software companies and hospitals are all rebuilding the apprenticeship schemes they had allowed to wither, and they are doing so, I would argue, for reasons that have nothing whatever to do with nostalgia. The first is simply supply. Employers report that they can find any number of candidates who can describe a process, and remarkably few who can actually carry it out. The second is retention. Someone trained inside a company, alongside colleagues who know their name, tends to stay; someone recruited on the strength of a certificate alone is far more likely to move on within a year. And the third reason, which is rarely stated aloud, is cost — for the employer, certainly, but also for the young person who leaves with a skill rather than a debt. None of this means that degrees are worthless. For a great many careers they remain essential. But it does suggest that our long habit of ranking one route above the other was never quite as sensible as it appeared.
