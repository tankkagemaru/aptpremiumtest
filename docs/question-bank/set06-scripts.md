### listening/set06-l3-q15.mp3
- **Voices:** one male and one female, 30s–40s, friendly disagreement between club members; the man firm and rule-minded, the woman thoughtful and evidence-led. About 90 seconds in total.
- **Pace:** B2, ~160 wpm, natural contractions, genuine back-and-forth.
- **Script:**
  - Man: Have you seen the proposal to make cycle helmets compulsory for everyone?
  - Woman: I have. And before you ask — yes, I always wear one. I've seen what a fall on to a kerb can do, and a helmet clearly takes a lot of the force out of a head injury.
  - Man: That's exactly my point. If it protects you, it should protect everybody. Make it the law.
  - Woman: That's where I hesitate. In the cities that have introduced a helmet law, the number of people riding at all has dropped sharply — by a quarter in some cases. Fewer riders means drivers grow less used to seeing bicycles, and the roads end up more dangerous, not less.
  - Man: I take the point, but I'd still fine anyone who ignored the rule. Otherwise the law means nothing at all.
  - Woman: I wouldn't go that far. Punishing someone who can't afford a thirty-pound helmet doesn't strike me as fair.
  - Man: Fair enough. Where I do agree with you completely is that none of this touches the real problem. A helmet does absolutely nothing about a lorry turning across a cycle lane.
  - Woman: Precisely. Build the lanes properly, redesign the junctions, and you prevent the crash instead of softening it. That has to come first.
  - Man: Agreed. Design first, headgear second.

### listening/set06-l4a-q16.mp3
- **Voices:** one female, 40s–50s, public-health researcher giving a talk; measured, wry, persuasive. About 110 seconds.
- **Pace:** C1, natural pace 160+ wpm, varied intonation, short pauses before the two rhetorical questions.
- **Script:** Ask most people whether it is healthier to cycle through the city or to drive through it, and they hesitate. Surely the cyclist, breathing hard beside a queue of exhaust pipes, takes in far more dirty air than the driver sealed inside a car? It is an intuition almost everybody shares, and the measurements simply do not support it. When researchers fit air-quality monitors to drivers and to riders on the same route, they find that concentrations of the worst pollutants are consistently higher inside the vehicle. The cabin traps the fumes of the car in front, and the ventilation system obligingly pumps them in. The cyclist, a metre to the side and slightly above the queue, is often in noticeably cleaner air. Now, I am not claiming that riding in heavy traffic is pleasant, or that we should stop worrying about roadside pollution. My point is about how we judge risk. We trust what we can see. The rider looks exposed, the driver looks protected, and a sheet of glass persuades us of something the instruments flatly deny. Any campaign that publishes the figures and then expects behaviour to change has, I think, misunderstood the problem. Before people will act on the evidence, they must first be shown why what they feel is wrong.

### listening/set06-l4b-q17.mp3
- **Voices:** one male, 40s, transport planner addressing a conference; dry, direct, faintly impatient. About 110 seconds.
- **Pace:** C1, natural pace 160+ wpm, emphasis on the three questions in the middle of the extract.
- **Script:** Every council that builds a cycle lane wants a number for its annual report, and the number it usually reaches for is the count of bicycles passing a sensor at eight in the morning. I want to suggest that this figure is close to useless. A lane can carry a great many riders and still be a failure, because a rush-hour count tells you nothing whatever about who is missing. Look carefully at those cyclists and a pattern emerges: they are overwhelmingly fit adults, in a hurry, wearing specialist equipment. These are the people who will ride whatever you build — through traffic, up hills, in the rain. Their presence measures their own determination, not the quality of your lane. The genuinely revealing questions are quite different ones. Are there twelve-year-olds riding to school on it, unaccompanied? Are there people in their seventies doing the shopping? Is anybody carrying a small child? Those riders are, if you like, the warning birds of the network: the first to disappear when a route begins to feel unsafe, and the last to come back. A city whose lanes are used only by the confident has not built a transport system at all. It has built a corridor for the brave — and then congratulated itself on the traffic count.
