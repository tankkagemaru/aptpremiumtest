### listening/set03-l4a-q16.mp3
- **Voices:** one female, 40s–50s, measured lecturer/food writer, engaged and reflective. About 110 seconds.
- **Pace:** C1, natural pace 160+ wpm, varied intonation.
- **Script:** Most of us grew up believing there were four basic tastes: sweet, sour, salty and bitter. Yet there is a fifth, one that Western science was surprisingly slow to accept. It's called umami, a Japanese word usually translated as 'savouriness'. Umami is the deep, mouth-filling taste you notice in ripe tomatoes, aged cheese, mushrooms and slow-cooked meat. A Japanese chemist first identified the compound responsible more than a century ago, but for decades many European researchers dismissed the idea, assuming umami was simply a combination of the other tastes. It was only when scientists located specific receptors on the tongue that respond to it that the debate was finally settled. What I find most interesting is not the chemistry itself, but what it tells us about how knowledge travels. A discovery made in one part of the world can be ignored elsewhere for generations, not because the evidence is weak, but because it doesn't fit the assumptions people already hold. Umami was hiding in plain sight in kitchens everywhere, and yet it took a shift in thinking, as much as a shift in technology, before it was finally recognised.

### listening/set03-l4b-q17.mp3
- **Voices:** one male, 40s, thoughtful commentator/columnist, gently persuasive. About 110 seconds.
- **Pace:** C1, natural pace 160+ wpm.
- **Script:** Food-delivery apps have transformed how city dwellers eat. With a few taps, dinner from almost any restaurant appears at your door within the hour. It's undeniably convenient, and for restaurants it can open up a whole new stream of customers. But I want to sound a note of caution. The commissions these platforms charge can swallow up to a third of a restaurant's takings on every order. For a small kitchen working on thin margins, that is enormous. Some owners tell me they feel trapped: they can't afford the fees, yet they can't afford to be the only restaurant in the street that isn't on the app either. There's a further, quieter effect. When we order from our sofas, we stop walking to the high street, and the casual life of the neighbourhood — the chance meetings, the passing trade for other shops — slowly fades. I'm not suggesting we abandon these apps; they're clearly here to stay. But I do think, as customers, we should occasionally ask who really pays for our convenience, and be willing now and then to walk to the restaurant ourselves.
