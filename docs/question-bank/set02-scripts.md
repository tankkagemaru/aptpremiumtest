### listening/set02-l4-q16.mp3
- **Voices:** one speaker, female 50s, measured lecturer's delivery
- **Pace:** B2+/C1, ~165 wpm, natural, with light emphasis on key contrasts
- **Script:** "Good evening. Tonight I want to challenge a comfortable assumption — that walking is always gentle on the natural world. It's true that a single walker leaves almost no trace. The difficulty arises with numbers. When a quiet hill becomes famous, perhaps through a single photograph shared online, visitor numbers can multiply tenfold in one season. The soil, never designed for such traffic, is stripped of its plants and washed away by the rain, leaving deep scars that can take decades to heal. Now, I'm not asking anyone to stay at home. Far from it — contact with wild places is precious, and the people who love the hills are exactly the people who will fight to protect them. My point is subtler. We need to spread our footsteps more widely, to celebrate the ordinary, unfashionable hills as well as the famous peaks. A landscape that everyone visits at once is a landscape under threat. If we truly value these places, we must sometimes have the discipline to choose the quieter path — and to encourage others to do the same."

### listening/set02-l4-q17.mp3
- **Voices:** one speaker, male 40s, engaging popular-science delivery
- **Pace:** B2+/C1, ~165 wpm, natural contractions
- **Script:** "Most of us walk to keep our bodies healthy — to strengthen the heart, to control our weight. What fascinates me, though, is what a long walk does to the mind. Researchers have noticed that people often solve their most stubborn problems not at their desks, but halfway up a hill. Why should this be? One idea is that the steady, repetitive rhythm of walking quietens the busy, anxious part of the brain, allowing looser, more creative connections to form. Add to that the open horizon, the absence of screens, and the simple fact that no one can email you on a mountain ridge, and you have ideal conditions for fresh thinking. I'd go further. I suspect that in our crowded, hurried lives, a walk is less a form of exercise than a form of rest — a rare chance for an overloaded mind to breathe. So the next time you feel stuck on a difficult question, don't reach for more coffee. Reach for your boots."

## Set 2 — Speaking photo specs

### speaking/set02-s2-hilltop-picnic.jpg
A colour photograph of a small group of friends (three or four adults, mixed genders, casual outdoor clothing) sitting on a grassy hilltop enjoying a picnic after a walk. The image must clearly show: rucksacks and walking boots set to one side, an open picnic of sandwiches, fruit and flasks spread on a cloth or flat rock, and a wide green landscape of hills or a valley stretching into the distance behind them. The people should look relaxed and happy, mid-conversation, with at least one holding food or a drink. Bright but soft daylight, natural setting, no text, signs, logos or recognisable branded products visible.

### speaking/set02-s3-mountain.jpg
A colour photograph of a single hiker walking along an open mountain or hillside trail in the fresh air. The image must clearly show the walker from behind or side-on wearing a rucksack and boots, a natural dirt or stone path, and dramatic open countryside — slopes, sky and perhaps distant peaks. The mood is free, healthy and outdoors. Daylight, no other people close by, no text, signs or branding visible. This image is paired with the gym photo below to contrast outdoor and indoor exercise.

### speaking/set02-s3-gym.jpg
A colour photograph of a person exercising indoors in a modern gym, for contrast with the mountain image. The image must clearly show the indoor setting: a treadmill or exercise machine, artificial lighting, mirrors or equipment racks in the background, and the person in sports clothing mid-workout. The mood is focused and urban. No text, brand names, or recognisable company logos should be visible. Frame it so the enclosed, indoor feel is obvious when placed next to the open mountain scene.
