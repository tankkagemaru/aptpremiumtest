### listening/set01-l4-q16.mp3
- Voice: one female, 40s–50s, neutral British accent, measured academic delivery.
- Pace: C1 ≈ 160+ wpm, natural contractions.
- Script:
> Good afternoon. Today I want to challenge a popular worry: the idea that smartphones are ruining our memories. It's often claimed that because we store phone numbers and facts on our devices, our brains have grown lazy. But the research paints a more interesting picture. What actually happens is that we remember less of the information itself, yet we become remarkably good at remembering where to find it. In other words, we're not losing memory; we're changing what we choose to remember. This isn't new, by the way. For centuries, people have used notebooks and libraries as external stores. The phone is simply a faster version of a very old habit. The real question is not whether we remember less, but whether we are thinking less deeply, and there, I must admit, the evidence is genuinely mixed.

## Set 1 — Speaking photo specs

### speaking/set01-s2-cinema-audience.jpg
A colour photograph taken inside a modern cinema during a screening. The shot should be from the front or side so that several rows of an audience are clearly visible, seated in tiered red or dark cinema seats and facing a large bright screen (the screen content should be neutral and out of focus — no recognisable film, logos or text). Show a mixed group of adults of different ages and appearances, some holding drinks or snack boxes; a couple of people can be smiling or talking quietly. Lighting should be dim but clear enough to see facial expressions and body language. The overall scene must obviously read as "people watching a film at the cinema" so a candidate can describe the place, the people and what they are doing. No readable brand names, film titles or on-screen text.
