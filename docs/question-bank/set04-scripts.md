### listening/set04-l2-q14.mp3
- **Voices:** four distinct speakers — Speaker A (male, 20s), Speaker B (female, 40s), Speaker C (female, 30s), Speaker D (male, 50s). Each speaks for about 25 seconds.
- **Pace:** B1, ~140 wpm, natural and reflective.
- **Script:**
  - Speaker A: I only really come alive once the sun has gone down. Wet pavements, shop signs, headlights reflected in a puddle — the whole city turns into colour. In daylight those same streets look completely ordinary to me, so I hardly ever bother taking my camera out before evening.
  - Speaker B: I walk for hours in the hills to find my pictures, and the walking is half the reason I go. What I'm really looking for is emptiness. If there's a car park and a queue of visitors at the viewpoint, I turn round and keep climbing until there's nobody else in the frame.
  - Speaker C: For me it's all about faces. I never take a picture of a stranger without speaking to them first — I'll spend ten minutes chatting at a market stall before I even lift the camera. By then they've relaxed, and you can see it. A photograph of somebody who trusts you looks completely different.
  - Speaker D: People assume you have to go somewhere exotic, but honestly, everything I shoot is within about three metres of my kitchen table. Steam rising off a cup, the shadow of a leaf on the wall. It costs me nothing, I don't need a ticket anywhere, and there is always something new if you look properly.

  - Woman: Absolutely.

### listening/set04-l4a-q16.mp3
- **Voices:** one female, 40s–50s, lecturer or technology writer; measured, thoughtful, lightly ironic. About 110 seconds.
- **Pace:** C1, natural pace 160+ wpm, varied intonation.
- **Script:** When you press the button on a modern phone, you are not really taking one photograph. In the fraction of a second before and after, the device captures a whole burst of frames, then merges them, removes what it decides is noise, brightens the shadows, sharpens the eyes and, in some cases, fills in detail that the sensor never actually received. What you are handed is not a recording of the light in front of you; it is a very confident piece of software's opinion of what that light probably meant. People find this disturbing. They ask whether such an image can still be called a photograph at all. My own view is that the question is less dramatic than it sounds, because the honest, untouched photograph was always something of a fiction. A film photographer chose the film, the exposure, the moment and the crop, and then a printer in a darkroom made a dozen further decisions about how dark a shadow should be. The interpretation used to happen slowly, in human hands. Now it happens instantly, inside a chip. The difference is real, but it is a difference of speed and of who is deciding — not the sudden loss of an innocence that photography never had in the first place.

### listening/set04-l4b-q17.mp3
- **Voices:** one male, 40s, travel writer addressing an audience; wry, unhurried, gently persuasive. About 110 seconds.
- **Pace:** C1, natural pace 160+ wpm.
- **Script:** There is a wooden footbridge in a valley I visited last summer. Ten years ago, perhaps three people a day crossed it. Last August I counted more than two hundred, and almost every one of them stopped at precisely the same spot, held up a phone at precisely the same angle, and walked away again within ninety seconds. They had all seen the same image online, and they had come to reproduce it. That, for me, is the uncomfortable part. It is not that people photograph their holidays; it is that a handful of pictures now decide where the crowds go, and everybody ends up queueing to take a picture that already exists. The village a mile down the road, which nobody has ever photographed well, sees no one at all. So my suggestion is a modest one. When you travel, spend a morning photographing the things that are not on anybody's list — the bus shelter, the man mending a net, the light on an ordinary wall. Those pictures will not collect much attention. They will, however, be yours, and years later they will be the ones that actually return you to the place.
