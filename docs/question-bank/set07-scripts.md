### listening/set07-l3-q15.mp3
- **Voices:** one female and one male, 30s–40s, friendly disagreement between colleagues; the man enthusiastic, the woman practical and questioning. About 90 seconds in total.
- **Pace:** B2, ~160 wpm, natural contractions and comfortable interruptions.
- **Script:**
  - Woman: Did you read that the education board wants music to be compulsory for every pupil until sixteen?
  - Man: I saw that, and I'm all for it. Music teaches you patience and teamwork in a way that very few other subjects do.
  - Woman: I'd agree with the teamwork part, actually. Playing in a group taught me more about listening to other people than any lesson ever did.
  - Man: Exactly my point.
  - Woman: My worry is where the time is supposed to come from. Something has to be dropped, and it's usually whichever subject the parents complain about least.
  - Man: That's fair, although I'd argue the timetable isn't nearly as full as head teachers pretend.
  - Woman: And instruments cost money. A school in a poorer area simply can't buy thirty violins.
  - Man: It doesn't have to. Singing costs nothing at all, and several of the countries with the finest music education begin with the voice and nothing else.
  - Woman: Hmm. I hadn't thought of that.
  - Man: What does worry me is forcing it on teenagers who genuinely hate it. Compulsory anything can kill the enjoyment.
  - Woman: Oh, absolutely. Make it a requirement and by fifteen half of them will never touch an instrument again.

### listening/set07-l4a-q16.mp3
- **Voices:** one female, 40s, psychologist giving a public talk; curious and precise, with dry humour. About 110 seconds.
- **Pace:** C1, natural pace 160+ wpm, varied intonation.
- **Script:** Almost everyone has had a tune lodge itself in their head and refuse to leave. Psychologists call these involuntary musical images, though most of us prefer the blunter word: earworms. The obvious explanation — that catchy songs are simply well written — turns out to be only a small part of the story. The tunes that stick tend to be fast, simple in shape, and yet they contain one small surprise: an unexpected leap, or an odd repetition that the brain cannot quite file away. In other words, an earworm is not a perfect melody but a slightly unfinished one. The mind, faced with a pattern it cannot complete, keeps replaying it in the hope of closing the loop. That would explain why the cure most people stumble on by accident actually works: listening to the whole song, right through to the final note, tends to release you, while trying hard not to think about the tune does precisely the opposite. What interests me most, though, is how neatly this fits a much broader principle. Memory, it seems, is not drawn to the smooth and the finished. It clings to the things that are almost, but not quite, resolved.

### listening/set07-l4b-q17.mp3
- **Voices:** one male, 40s–50s, music journalist addressing an audience; wry, gently persuasive, never preachy. About 110 seconds.
- **Pace:** C1, natural pace 160+ wpm.
- **Script:** Ask most people how they discover new music now, and they will point at an algorithm. The recommendation engine has become the friend who never runs out of suggestions, and I use it as much as anyone. But I want to put a slightly awkward question. What exactly is a recommendation engine optimising for? Not, I would suggest, your musical growth. It is optimising for the next click — for the track you are least likely to skip. And the safest way to stop you skipping is to hand you something reassuringly close to what you already like. The result is a strange kind of comfort. Your listening becomes smoother, more agreeable, and quietly narrower. The discoveries that once changed people — the record a cousin pushed into your hands, the band you sat through simply because you had already paid — were uncomfortable precisely because nobody had calculated that you would enjoy them. Now, I am not calling for anyone to switch these services off; that ship has sailed. I am suggesting something smaller and rather harder: that once a month you choose something deliberately unlike yourself, sit through all of it, and see what happens.
